Investigating Existing Systems: A Designer's Guide

Become a design detective to uncover a user's story.

Section 1: The Research Methods

As a designer-detective, your first job is to understand your user's world. This is called user research. Your goal is to fill out the "human swim lane" of your User Journey Map. Here are three primary methods you can use to gather information.

1. The Interview

This is the most direct way to get insights. You get to hear the user's story in their own words.

How to do it:

  • Ask open-ended questions like, "Walk me through your routine..."
  • Listen carefully and take notes.

Capturing Data:

  • Record the interview (with permission).
  • Create a **mind map** of their responses to visually organise key themes.

2. The Observation

Sometimes people do things without thinking about them. Observe your user's process to uncover key details.

How to do it:

  • Watch your user as they perform a task.
  • Look for moments of hesitation or frustration.

Capturing Data:

  • Record a video and use key clips.
  • Take a series of photos and **storyboard** the process, annotating each key moment.

3. The Diary Study

Capture a user's experience over a longer period of time by having them document their actions and thoughts.

How to do it:

  • Provide a notebook or a digital form.
  • Ask them to log their experience with the memory problem over a few days or a week.

Capturing Data:

  • The user's entries themselves are your data.
  • Present excerpts from their diary as direct quotes or summarise key trends in a brief report.

Section 2: The Forgetful Gardener - Examples

Here are some specific examples using our persona, "Graceful Gwen," to show you how these methods work in practice. Use your findings to fill out your research slide.

1. Interview Example

  • Research Question: How does a home gardener currently remember when to water their plants?
  • Research Method: Interview
  • Key Insight: The gardener relies on their memory and a few scattered notes, but feels a sense of guilt when they forget, highlighting an emotional pain point.

2. Observation Example

  • Research Question: What is the physical process a gardener follows to water multiple plants?
  • Research Method: Observation
  • Key Insight: A video or storyboard of the gardener shows a tedious process of walking back and forth between plants, physically checking the soil, and looking for lost notes. This uncovers the pain point of tedious manual checks.

3. Diary Study Example

  • Research Question: What emotional and practical issues does a gardener face when trying to remember their watering schedule?
  • Research Method: Diary Study
  • Key Insight: A diary entry might reveal the gardener's anxiety and hope, stating, "I put a sticky note on the pot to remind me to water it on Saturday. I hope I remember." This shows that their current system is unreliable, leading to anxiety and a lack of trust.

Happy sleuthing!
